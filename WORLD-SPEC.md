# World Spec v0.2 — THE DRIED SEA (working title)

*All proper names are working titles — placeholders to be taste-tested. The structure is the spec; the names are paint.*

## Premise

Three generations ago, the sea left. Not receded — *left*, in a single night the songs call the Withdrawal. What remains is the world's largest grave: a seabed continent of salt flats, petrified reefs, wreck fields from a thousand years of drowned shipping, and — at the far, dark edge — the Trench, where the last of the water and the worst of what lived in it went down together.

The sea gods did not die. Worse: they diminished. A pantheon built on an ocean now runs on the memory of one. They still answer prayers — but every answer costs them, and there is not much left.

You are a descendant of the coast peoples, scraping a living on the dead seabed. You will build a village from wreck-timber, gather the scattered survivors, raise a church to a fading god, and walk down into the dark after the two questions everyone stopped asking a generation ago: *where did the sea go — and can it come back?*

### Why the gods are weak (the mystery spine)
The pantheon's power **was** the sea. Player-facing lore reveals in layers: (L1, ambient) the gods weakened with the Withdrawal; (L2, priests + found texts) the pantheon has a *missing member* — the All-Tide, the sea itself, of whom the others were aspects; (L3, at the Drowned Priest) what actually happened that night — and that the empty seat **can be filled, or emptied forever**. Bosses are keyed to this spine (see below), and the spine ends at a player verdict (next section).

## The Verdict — the endgame you determine

*The Baldur's Gate principle, survival-game-shaped: the story is decided by **deeds, not dialogue**. No karma meter — the game reads the ledgers you were already keeping.*

### How the game knows who you are
Three systemic tells, all recorded by systems that exist anyway:
1. **The devotion ledger** — the worship loop is secretly the morality system: *do you give back what you take?* Vigor drained vs. worship returned, per god, over the whole game. A player who only takes is already choosing.
2. **God-remnants** — each boss and god-vault yields a remnant (a divine fragment). At every one: **enshrine it** (strengthens a god), **trade it** (neutral), or **consume it** (keep the power for yourself — the ascension tell; Ur-Noth's voice gets warmer each time).
3. **How you shepherd** — which chapels stand in your village, whether an Unlit "priest" lives among your people, which rites you've led, and the Shepherd's ledger (tribesmen section): how your people were treated, and whether your justice had evidence behind it.

Small **echo choices** through all three biomes (a shrine you could restore or strip; a Drowned soul you could lay to rest or bind) keep the verdict *earned* rather than sprung — the BG feel comes from the accumulation, not the finale.

### The fork: the Drowned Priest (EA finale)
At the bottom of The Drop, the All-Tide's last high priest has held one rite for three generations — a thread of the sea, not yet severed. Reaching him is the EA climax; what you do there opens one of three paths (full paths are the post-EA act, but **the choice itself ships in EA** — the cliffhanger is *your own decision*):

| Path | The deed at the fork | The campaign it opens | The ending | The cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **The Tidebringer** | Join the rite | Restore each god to full Vigor (pilgrimages to their drowned sanctums — the worship loop at campaign scale) and gather the pantheon to finish the calling | **The sea comes home.** Over everything. Including your village — the finale is preparing your people and your works for the flood you prayed for | You drown the world you built to save the world that was |
| **The Unlit Compact** | Sever the thread — with Ur-Noth's blessing | Hunt the diminished gods one by one and feed them to the dark; your priests desert or turn; the Unlit congregation moves in | **An eternal, thriving dark.** The dry world becomes Ur-Noth's kingdom and you its first prophet — powerful, dreaded, and never again alone in the dark | Every chapel you raised becomes a tomb you visit |
| **The Ascension** | Take the thread for yourself | Drain every god — including, last and hardest, Ur-Noth — consuming remnants until the empty seat recognizes you | **You become the All-Tide** — and choose, in the final scene, what kind of sea (or no sea) the world gets. The player literally writes the epilogue | To hold the throne you must betray every god who ever answered you — and your own priests conduct your apotheosis or die refusing |

### The Tidebringer ending, in full (design detail)
Committing (a marked point of no return after restoring all gods via sanctum pilgrimages) starts **the Ark phase**: N in-game days before the sea arrives. The final challenge is an **evacuation, not a boss** — move people, herds, relics, and legendaries to the old coastline rim and raise the shore-settlement; wagon space is a real decision; tribesmen make personal asks (disposition-driven); folk saved by your Deep Callings arrive to help. The flood mirrors the Withdrawal: the sea left in one night and returns in one night — watched from the rim as your old village's lights go out under the water. Revelation: **the sea IS the All-Tide** — restoring the ocean resurrects the missing god; the pantheon is whole, the Drowned rest, the trench-things sink home. **Playable epilogue** at the shore-settlement: peace-tuned world, full-Vigor gods, the drowned village visible beneath clear water on calm days, and fishing on the returned sea as the post-game loop. Sequel hook: there is an ocean now, and boats being built.

### The endings in co-op — teams ascend and rule together
Every path must be walkable by a party as a party (requirement, 2026-07). Committing to a path is a group rite — all present players take part (dissenters trigger the Schism instead).

- **Tidebringer** is co-op-native already: the Ark phase is a group evacuation, and the rim-watch is shared. Trivial.
- **The Unlit Compact, team version: Ur-Noth's court.** The party rules the eternal dark *together* — the campaign's god-hunts are group content, and the epilogue seats each player in an **office of the dark** read from their deed-ledger: the Voice (who did the preaching/rumors), the Hand (who did the killing), the Eye (who found what was hidden), the Keeper (who held the village). Playable dark epilogue: the village is now a citadel, tribesmen serve or fear by their disposition history, and the world's remaining lights are yours to permit.
- **The Ascension, team version: the new pantheon.** One empty seat can't hold four friends — so a team that consumes the gods doesn't fill the throne, **it replaces the pantheon**. Each player ascends into a domain the game *reads from their whole campaign* — the ledgers know who fought like a storm, who kept the hearth, who walked at night — producing personalized apotheoses (BG3-epilogue energy, data-driven and cheap: domain = f(deed ledgers)). The All-Tide's seat itself: the group chooses one to take it, shares it, or — the poetic option — leaves it empty, and the new gods inherit the old mystery. Solo play keeps the original: you take the whole throne alone.
- Epilogue rule for both dark paths: your tribesmen become your first clergy — or your first martyrs — according to how you shepherded them. The Shepherd's ledger writes the last page.

### World-state reactivity (scoped honestly)
Reactivity budget = **systemic flags, not authored branches**: priest dialogue pools keyed to your verdict-lean; weather/spawn shifts as gods strengthen or fail (Maren restored → storms gentler for you; a god consumed → their domain effects die world-wide, permanently — *consuming the Tide-Keeper breaks the tide-rhythm buffs for everyone, forever*); path-exclusive legendaries (each path locks ~2 of the game's best artifacts, BG-style build-around temptation). What we do NOT promise: companion-romance-grade dialogue reactivity, per-NPC memory, or branch-unique biomes. Three paths share one world; the world changes *state*, not *shape*.

### The Quiet Ascension — the traitor in the village (REVISED per Owen's critique, 2026-07)
*Original concept — a player secretly walking the dark path in persistent co-op — was cut on Owen's analysis: hidden-traitor design needs short rounds and big groups; in a 3-friend, 80-hour campaign the suspect pool is trivial, the secret PvP turn is non-consensual, and a secretly-snowballing player is unbalanceable. What survives is the fantasy itself, relocated to where it works:*

**The traitor is an NPC — and betrayal is not a script, it's the far end of the disposition system** (tribesmen section). On any server (and in single-player — this needs no multiplayer at all), Ur-Noth recruits from your *village*: a tribesman or priest — someone you rescued, ranked up, trusted with your forge — drifts there through neglect, grievance, susceptible history, and your choices (hosting an Unlit priest raises the odds). Left entirely to their own devices, they escalate on their own timeline — from stolen remnants all the way to acts that weaken the gods and, at the limit, tilt the Verdict itself.

- **The paranoia engine ports directly:** consumed remnants and dark rites still weaken gods world-wide; blessings flicker; priests mutter that *someone among us takes and does not give back*. A remnant goes missing from the offering chest. Your warden was seen walking toward the Drop at night. The evidence is real, ambiguous, and now safely aimed at NPCs.
- **Counterplay unchanged:** the **Rite of Revelation** (church tier 3, expensive) confirms *whether* the dark walks among you, not who. Watch schedules, check the ledgers, confront suspects — a village whodunit built from the systems' own bookkeeping. Accuse wrong and you lose an innocent villager's loyalty (or the villager).
- **The payoff scales with investment:** an undiscovered traitor's apotheosis attempt turns your highest-ranked, best-equipped villager — wearing the gear *you* forged them — into a campaign boss. The "final boss is someone you trusted" moment survives; it's just someone the *game* made you trust instead of someone you drove here to play with.
- **The Schism (design note, P2+):** if a co-op group *wants* player-vs-player endings, the fork supports it **openly** — players may declare different Verdict paths at the Drowned Priest, and the endgame becomes a declared rivalry (race or war, both sides knowing). No secrecy, no consent problem: schism is a choice made in front of everyone, which is also just better drama.
- **Scope win:** the NPC version is *cheaper* than the player version (no private-state netcode, no social-balance tuning), works in single-player, and ships as part of the tribesman system's natural growth rather than a separate flagship mode.

### Scope note
This is systems + data + a handful of scripted moments — squarely in AI's strong lanes. The expensive line-items are the three ending cinematics, the fork scene, and Quiet-Ascension balance tuning; everything else is DataTable flags and dialogue pools. The Verdict is P1 only as far as the fork scene (EA needs the choice); the three path campaigns and the Quiet Ascension are the post-EA act structure.

## The Pantheon of the Drowned (patron system = the class system)

**You may worship many gods.** There is no class picker and no exclusive patron — your "build" is *how you split your devotion across the pantheon*, and it is fully respeccable:

- **Devotion** is a finite, growable budget (earned through play milestones — bosses, biome firsts, rites led). Attuning to a god costs devotion per **rank** (1–3): rank 1 unlocks the first invocation + a faint blessing; rank 3 is the god's full kit and strongest passive.
- **Depth vs. breadth is the whole game:** 6 gods × 3 ranks ≫ your budget (~4–5 ranks affordable at EA). One-deep-one-shallow, or three-shallow — the classic talent tradeoff, but diegetic.
- You still **find each god in the world** (kneel at their fallen shrine to open attunement) — discovery stays a moment, it just doesn't slam the other doors.
- **Respec = the Rite of Rededication** at any church: shift devotion between gods for offerings + a short time-gate. Deliberately *cheap enough to encourage exploring builds* — the cost is ceremony, not punishment. (Exception: walking away from **Ur-Noth** costs more. He remembers.)
- Organic balance note: each attuned god has their **own Vigor pool** and needs their **own worship time and infrastructure** (below) — so breadth buys more total emergency casts, but every pool is shallower and your village works harder. It self-balances through the systems rather than a rulebook.

Each god defines: **2–3 clutch invocations** (break-glass magic, never a rotation), **one passive blessing**, **a priest type + church aesthetic**, and **an affinity artifact line** (Track 2 legendaries requiring rank-2+ attunement).

### Gear leads the build (the found-armor pull)
The intended respec driver is **loot, not advice**: legendary finds are build-defining and attunement-affine, so pulling the ultimate tank armor out of a deep wreck *is* the game suggesting you tank. Rededication is priced cheap precisely so that moment works — you found the Salt-Father's Bulwark, of course you're going to rebuild around it. Design rule: seed Track-2 finds (and their rumors) across *all* the gods' affinity lines regardless of the player's current build, so the world keeps handing you reasons to become someone else.

| God (working name) | Domain | Clutch invocations (examples) | Passive blessing | Fantasy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Maren, the Storm-Mother** | Storm, lightning, rage | Call a squall (AoE knockdown + lightning); Wind-wall (projectile denial) | Storm-sense (weather warning); stamina regen in rain/storms | The angry one. War-priest energy |
| **Neris, the Tide-Keeper** | Tides, time, return | Slack Tide (brief time-slip — escape or reposition); The Returning Wave (major heal-over-time) | Tide-rhythm: buffs oscillate on a day cycle you learn to ride | The patient one. Sustain/clutch-survival |
| **Halor, the Salt-Father** | Preservation, endurance, hearth | Pillar of Salt (self/ally petrify — invulnerable, rooted); Brine-ward (structure shield) | Food never spoils; resistances; village/tribesman morale bonus | The homestead god. Builder/defender |
| **Vessa, the Current-Runner** | Currents, travel, cunning | Rip-current (dash through enemies, carries allies); Undertow (yank a target to you / away) | Carry weight, move speed, fall damage reduction | The explorer's god. Mobility/logistics |
| **Old Ghal, the Leviathan-Shepherd** | Beasts, the hunt | The Shepherd's Voice (pacify/turn a beast — even, briefly, a boss's adds); Blood-scent (track anything) | Taming affinity; beasts hesitate before attacking you | The wild one. Tamer/hunter |
| **Ur-Noth, the Unlit** | The abyss, the dark, hunger | Devouring Dark (fear/blind AoE); Anglerlight (false lure — enemies attack it); the strongest magic in the game | See in darkness; the dark biomes fear you a little | The forbidden patron — see below |
| **THE ALL-TIDE** | The sea itself | — | — | **Missing.** The empty seat. Endgame material |

**Ur-Noth is the Conan-corruption homage, relocated:** the Unlit is the one god the Withdrawal *strengthened* — the trench-dark god who doesn't drain like the others. Ur-Noth's magic draws on **you** as well as the god (max-HP scarring, visual transformation), and Ur-Noth's "priests" are... not comfortable people to host in your village. Strongest invocations, permanent costs, social consequences. The dark-class option for players who want it, opt-in and marked.

### The Vigor economy (magic system core)

- Each god has a **Vigor pool** — small, precious, slow. Invocations spend it in big chunks; two or three casts and your god is spent.
- As Vigor falls, **your passive blessing visibly dims** (weaker → gone), and at empty the god goes **Dormant**: no magic, no blessing, and your priest is in visible distress. The world should make you *feel* it — dimmed shrine-light, the god's voice faint.
- **Recovery is worship:** casting spends a dying god's strength; **worship gives it back**. Return to that god's church and lead or join rites with your priest — **time-gated** (in-game days), the Conan-dancer pattern reframed as restoration, not apology. You are feeding a weakened god back to life, and the tone should carry that: gratitude, not guilt. Recovery speed scales with church tier and priest rank; **offerings** (rare, exploration-sourced goods — trench pearls, storm-glass, first-wreck relics) buy the rate down. You cannot buy an instant refill; the gate is the point. With multiple gods attuned, worship time is *per god* — the real cost of a broad build.
- Design intent: magic saves your life **once per pickle**, roughly. If a player casts daily, we've tuned it wrong. The satisfaction is Conan's: *I had one miracle in my pocket, and I spent it at exactly the right moment.*
- **Emergency overdraw** (v2 candidate): at zero Vigor you may cast anyway — once — by burning something that doesn't come back (permanent Vigor cap loss, or a scar on the god the world remembers). For the moment when the pickle is *that* bad.

### The Works — every god has a build set (spells drain; works feed)
Beyond a god-neutral tier-0 set (driftwood, wreck-timber, rope — nobody builds *nothing*), **every buildable belongs to a god**, and each god's works express their domain:
- **Halor:** the homestead heart — hearths, smokehouses, salt-cellars, granaries, thick walls.
- **Maren:** storm-craft — lightning-rods (real storm protection), storm-cisterns, wind-shutters, bellows.
- **Neris:** tide and time — tide-bells, water-clocks, healing baths, rest and recovery pieces.
- **Vessa:** movement — roads, bridges, carts, way-beacons, porters' posts.
- **Old Ghal:** the beast-yard — pens, stables, taming posts, kennels.
- **Ur-Noth:** the grim works. The Salt-Wheel, the rendering-vat, the drain-pumps, the lightless lures — *that's whose set that is*, and it always was.

**Use is worship.** Building a god's piece earns a pulse of favor; a piece *in use* feeds its god a slow standing trickle (the smokehouse curing meat is a small prayer to Halor; the Salt-Wheel grinding a captive feeds Ur-Noth with every turn — convenience as accumulating corruption). Idle pieces feed nothing — favor can't be spam-built, only lived.

**Favor tiers gate set depth:** build and genuinely use a god's works and their deeper recipes open — which creates honest material pull toward the gods whose tools fit your life, independent of your devotion build. A player who never attunes to Halor but cooks, cures, and walls like a homesteader is *still* courting him, and Halor notices. The village-as-character-sheet gets its final layer: your architecture is not just a confession, it's a **congregation** — every working building in it is quietly praying to someone.

- Progression: **fallen shrine** (found in-world, activates patron) → **chapel** (built, unlocks a wandering priest arrival) → **church** (priest rank 2, faster rites, offering altar) → **basilica** (endgame; pantheon-level rites).
- **Per-god infrastructure:** rank 1 attunement needs only that god's activated shrine; rank 2 needs a chapel + priest in your village; rank 3 needs a full church. A broad build literally reshapes your village — a street of chapels — while a devoted build raises one great basilica. Your base *is* your character sheet, readable from a distance.
- **Priests are attracted, not built:** raise a god's chapel, keep an offering lit, and within days a wandering priest of that god arrives — Soulmask-style recruitment, flavored as pilgrimage. Priests are tribesmen with a class: they conduct restoration rites and Rededication, cleanse Ur-Noth scarring (if you dare host both), buff the village, and **sell rumors** (see itemization).
- Pantheon politics (v2): tribesmen have their own devotions; certain god pairs bless or friction with each other under one palisade. Homestead-scale theology.

## Biomes (EA target: 3, by depth band)

Progression = **descent**. Elevation reads as time: the higher the seabed, the longer it's been dry, the safer it is. Going deeper is going backward into the drowning.

### 1. The Salt Shallows (starter)
Blinding white flats, salt pillars, beached fishing fleets, brine pools (hazard — the only "water" left, and it burns). The bones of the coast towns.
- **Resources:** driftwood, wreck-timber, salt (food preservation + trade), bronze salvage, ship-cloth.
- **Threats:** salt-hounds (pack hunters), scuttle-crabs, dust-eels (ambush from the flats), desperate raiders (human).
- **Boss: Old Shellback** — a crab the size of a chapel wearing a hull as a shell. Teaches the game's boss grammar: preparation, arena, weak-point.
- **Signature legendary hunt:** the *Lighthouse-Keeper's Lantern* (never goes out; the dark biomes respect it).

### 2. The Reef Forest (mid)
Petrified coral as canopy — a forest where every tree is a dead animal. Muted purples and bone-whites; anemone groves that still move when nothing else does; drowned towns tangled in the reef. Permanent twilight under the canopy.
- **Resources:** coralwood (tier-2 timber), reef-iron (tier-2 metal), pearl, dye, anemone silk.
- **Threats:** urchin-backs, eel-wolves, the Drowned (dead sailors who don't know the sea left — night surface-walkers), angler-stalkers (the canopy dark).
- **Boss: The Anglermother** — the light in the deep wood that has been calling your tribesmen in their dreams. Fight in the dark; her lure is the only light, and you need it to see.
- **Signature legendary hunt:** *Neris's Hourglass* (recipe in three fragments across drowned town chapels).

### 3. The Drop (deep — EA finale)
The trench edge. The land tilts and the light gives up; bioluminescence is the only color. The oldest wrecks — first-age ships, pre-Withdrawal god-vaults — and the last brine, pooled in the dark. Ur-Noth's country.
- **Resources:** tidebrass (tier-3 metal, working name), storm-glass, trench pearl, god-vault relics.
- **Threats:** things with lures, things with too many joints, the Unlit's congregation (part faith, part fauna).
- **Boss: The Drowned Priest** — the All-Tide's last high priest, still holding a rite that has been running for three generations. Not (only) a fight: reaching him is the EA climax and **the Verdict fork** (see The Verdict) — L3 lore lands here, and the player's choice is the cliffhanger.
- **Post-EA:** the Trench proper descends from here — **dry-dark all the way down** (brine-slick, bioluminescent, never swimmable), ending at **the Last Sea**: the final cup of the world's ocean, black and still, where whatever remains of the pantheon's true power pools. All three Verdict campaigns converge on its shore with different deeds — call the sea home from it, feed it to Ur-Noth, or drink the throne. Canon: water is the holiest and most lethal substance in the world; brine burns because it is god-charged. You kneel beside the Last Sea; you do not swim it.

### World rhythm: the Storm
Dry-world weather, but the old sea weather never fully left. Periodic **great storms**: danger up everywhere, Maren's magic half-cost, and — the loop driver — **the seabed shifts**: new wrecks exposed, leviathan carcasses beach (harvest events + Old Ghal content), rumor-map refreshed. Storms are the world's loot-table reroll, on a schedule you can feel coming (storm-sense makes Maren followers everyone's favorite neighbor).

## Itemization: two tracks

**Track 1 — the Tree (Valheim-fair):** deterministic tech tree. Salvage-bronze → reef-iron → tidebrass. Everything progression needs is reliably craftable from biome-gated materials. No one is ever walled by luck.

**Track 2 — the Legends (the hunt):** named artifacts. Recipes are **never on the tree** — they exist as fragments, rumors, and hunts:
- **Sources:** rumor purchases from priests/travelers, found sea-charts, god-vaults, storm-exposed wrecks, boss-adjacent secrets (never boss-required).
- **Structure per legend:** recipe (1–3 fragments, placed not rolled) → materials (at least one from an *event* — storm carcass, seasonal spawn, deep expedition) → **ritual craft** at a god-touched station (an occasion, with witnesses if you have a village).
- **Design laws:** always optional, always worth it, rare-because-the-journey-is-long (no lottery drops), every legend is *named* and has a paragraph of history, and each patron's affinity line means no single character chases them all — trade and multi-character replay value.
- **Examples to set tone:** the *Lighthouse-Keeper's Lantern*; *Maren's Own Harpoon* (calls the bolt down on a hit); the *Salt-Father's Hearthstone* (a village heart that ends spoilage in a radius); *the Unlit Crown* (Ur-Noth line; costs what you'd expect).

## The Last Peoples — save them, or push them over the brink

*The dried sea has peoples older and stranger than the coast-folk, peaceful and barely holding on. They are the game's macro morality: whole communities whose survival is, honestly, optional — and whose wealth is real.*

### The peoples (EA ships two; working designs)
- **The Reefkin** (Reef Forest) — beings who grew *with* the living reef and are petrifying as it dies; gentle, slow, half-coral, their elders already statues in the groves. Here is the razor the world hands you: **their bodies are the resource** — pearl, coralwood, reef-iron seed all come easiest from Reefkin dead. Save them by restoring reef-pools and warding their groves, and they are the finest craftfolk left alive. Push them — or simply *let* them — over the brink, and you strip-mine a graveyard of a gentle people.
- **The Brinefolk** (Salt Shallows / the Drop's edge) — salt-veined descendants who survived by drinking god-charged brine; they keep the last pools, and their distilled god-water is **the best worship offering in the game** (the Vigor economy runs through them). Trade fairly and protect their pools, and your gods drink well forever. Or drain the pools out from under them — a windfall of offerings, a people gone in a season.
- *(P2: the Shellwrights — hermit smallfolk in the wreck-fields, hoarding drowned-age salvage and recipe fragments; physically defenseless, catastrophically wealthy.)*

### The Brink (the mechanic)
Every settlement has a **Brink state** — population, light, song; readable by walking through, no UI meter needed at distance. The dying world pushes them toward the edge *passively*: doing nothing is itself a choice, and the game counts it once you've seen them and understood (no penalty for peoples you haven't met).
- **Save:** invest protection (raids target them), food, works (a warded grove, a pool-cistern), rites. Costly, ongoing, and slow — a real project, not a donation box.
- **Trade:** the honest middle. Fair prices, both sides better off, no ledger weight either way.
- **Exploit:** the spectrum matters — outright raiding, extortionate "protection," or the insidious option: quietly redirect their water, claim their salvage rights, and let the world do the killing while your hands look clean. **The ledgers read outcomes and causes, not just sword-strokes** — engineered decline weighs the same as massacre, and priests are not fooled.

### What each choice is worth (both must be real)
- **Saved peoples** are permanent assets: unique trade lines, unique tribesman recruits (Reefkin crafter, Brinefolk stillmaster — new classes), a stream of unique Callings, and **they show up at the end** — hands at the Ark evacuation, subjects who kneel or resist in the dark epilogues, faces on the rim in every finale.
- **Extinguished peoples** are a real windfall: their hoards, their land as buildable territory, materials in one season that honest play earns in five — plus Ur-Noth's warm approval and a permanent hole in the world: silent groves, dry pools, vengeance Callings from survivors, and gods who dim toward you (Halor especially). The Compact and Ascension paths genuinely *want* this wealth — that's what makes it a decision and not a test with an obvious answer.

### Verdict integration
The Last Peoples are the fourth ledger — the macro-scale of "do you give back what you take," now asked about communities instead of gods and villagers. Their fate is written into every epilogue.

*The pool is vast; your draw is yours. Instead of 100 shared quests everyone grinds identically, a deep pool (design ceiling 1000+; see production note) deals each character their own ~100 over a campaign. In co-op, your quest log and your friend's barely overlap.*

### Delivery is diegetic — no quest boards, no exclamation marks
Callings arrive as the world would send them: **god-dreams** (your patrons have work for you — devotion shapes what's asked), **the dead** (letters and last wishes on the drowned and the dried), **your tribesmen** (a salvager's confession, a priest's doubt — disposition generates asks), **sea-charts and rumors** (bought, found, overheard). The journal is a keepsake ledger, not a checklist.

### The draw is weighted, not random-random
Each character's pool draw is seeded by: devotion spread (Maren sends storm-work; host an Unlit priest and stranger letters find you), biome progress, playstyle tells, and village composition. Within a server, draws avoid duplication — **your knowledge is genuinely yours**, which makes co-op conversation into gameplay: "my dream said something's under the west flats" is information your friend literally cannot have.

### Depth tiers (and the secret weight)
- **Vignettes** — one scene, one name, one twist. Small but never generic (see laws).
- **Chains** — multi-step arcs across biomes and days.
- **Deep Callings** — the rare ones with **permanent world consequences**: a spring unsealed, a beast extinct, a god measurably strengthened, a POI transformed — per-server-once, and **never labeled in advance**. The magic moment is discovering the "little" favor you did in week two is why the tide-bell rings now. Some deals a player their shot at affecting everyone's world; distribution guarantees every player draws a few.

### The anti-shallow laws (each calling must pass all five)
1. **A name and a wound:** every calling involves someone specific — named, with a history rooted in the Withdrawal. No anonymous "a villager needs…".
2. **A turn:** at least one choice, twist, or moment where the quest isn't what it appeared.
3. **An echo:** completing it changes *something* observable — a dialogue line, a flag, a grave that gets flowers. Nothing vanishes on turn-in.
4. **Verbs wear clothes:** kill/fetch/escort are allowed only inside a story that justifies them. Never a bare counter.
5. **It can look you in the eye:** if it reads like filler at review, it dies. No quota rescues a dead quest.

### Verdict integration
Callings are the **delivery mechanism for echo choices** — many are small morality tests in plain clothes (the widow's request has a cruel efficient answer and a costly kind one), feeding the deed ledgers without ever announcing themselves as Choices™.

### Production note (the honest numbers)
The pool is schema-driven data — AI drafts against the quest-graph schema at volume; **every calling passes a human curation gate** (law #5 is Jeff, reading). Realistic targets: **EA ships ~250–300 curated callings** (already 2–3× a typical survival game's authored content), pool grows toward 1000+ post-EA as a standing content pipeline. This system is the purest expression of the project's thesis: the schema and laws are designed once at high effort; the volume is cheap-model work forever after.

## Tribesmen (the Soulmask homage, promoted to P1)

Classes: **salvager, smith, brinewife (cook/alchemist), warden (guard), reef-runner (scout/gatherer), priest (per god)** (+ saved-Peoples uniques). Rank/skill growth by doing. Assignment loop Soulmask-style: stations, schedules, expeditions-lite (send a salvage team to a marked wreck; they return with goods and, sometimes, trouble).

### Two doors into the village: the Rescued and the Taken
Every villager arrives through one of two doors — same disposition system underneath, opposite starting states:

- **The Rescued** — freed from raider camps, found half-mad in the reef, pilgrims drawn to your chapels, Last-Peoples folk you saved. They arrive with **faith high and grievance low**: loyal by gratitude, productive by choice, and they hold your village's culture together (their morale is the contagion that counters drift).
- **The Taken** — captured, not recruited (the Conan lane): subdue a raider, bind them, walk them home to the **yoke-post**. They work under warden watch from day one — **productivity now, poison later**: obedience is fear-shaped, grievance starts high, susceptibility is maxed, and **Ur-Noth recruits from the resentful** — a yard full of captives is the disposition system's easiest tinder. Escape attempts, sabotage, and dark whispers all live here.
- **Origin is a door, not a destiny.** A captive treated well — fed like family, granted shrine access, eventually offered the **Rite of Unbinding** — can convert into your most loyal villager (the redemption arc: they know exactly what you could have done instead). A rescued villager neglected long enough sours like anyone else.
- **The Shepherd's ledger reads both who they were and how you hold them:** yoking raiders who burned your outbuildings is justice-adjacent; keeping broken innocents in chains is tyranny; the Unbinding is one of the game's quietest good deeds. The dark paths genuinely *want* captive labor (it's cheap and fast, and Compact/Ascension epilogues have a place for overseers) — priced, as always, in what the gods think of you and in what your yard full of resentment eventually does.
- Warden class gains its real job: watching the Taken. Understaff it and the disposition math does what disposition math does.

### Grim works — recipes you may not want to build
Conversion of the Taken has **two routes, and only one needs an evil instrument**:
- **The Breaking** (fast, dark): build **the Salt-Wheel** (working name — the wheel-of-pain descendant) and captives grind quickly into **the Broken** — obedient, productive, dim: no faith growth, no true loyalty, forever susceptible. Scales beautifully; costs what it costs.
- **The Unbinding** (slow, kind): good treatment + time + the rite. Produces true villagers, needs no instrument — only patience and warden coverage while you wait.

The Salt-Wheel is the founding member of a small class of **grim recipes** — buildables with real utility that a decent player never has cause to craft: the Salt-Wheel, an Ur-Noth shrine, the rendering-vat (consume remnants at better yield), brine-drain pumps and the reef-saw (industrialize exploiting the Last Peoples). Collectively these are **Ur-Noth's build set** (see The Works — every buildable belongs to a god, and use feeds its owner). Design rules:
1. **The game never requires one.** Every grim work has a slower, kinder alternative. Rescue-only players go the whole game with a build menu they can be proud of — the *absence* is the reward.
2. **Recipes arrive corruptly** — from Ur-Noth's whispers, a raider chief's notes, a slaver's wreck. Knowing how to build the thing is itself a stain with a story.
3. **Structures testify.** The ledgers read what stands in your village, and so does everyone who visits: priests won't walk past the Wheel without muttering, pilgrims arrive slower, saved Peoples refuse to trade inside sight of it, and Ur-Noth's voice warms with every grim work raised. The village-as-character-sheet now includes its confessions — a visitor can read your morality from your skyline as surely as your build.

### Traits — no two villagers are the same problem
Every tribesman generates with **2–4 traits** across four axes, plus their hidden disposition:
- **Faith:** devout (of a *specific* god) / observant / agnostic / bitter. Devout villagers need access to their god's shrine and — the load-bearing loop — **a devout village worships passively: your villagers' faith is a standing trickle of Vigor restoration**. Agnostics contribute nothing to the gods but are **harder for Ur-Noth to whisper to** (faith is the vector; you can't corrupt a belief that isn't there). Neither is the "correct" hire.
- **Work:** industrious / steady / idle / burst-worker (useless on schedules, brilliant in a crisis).
- **Social:** kind, surly, storyteller (a fire-side buff to everyone's morale), loner, gossip (spreads doubt *and* surfaces secrets).
- **Quirks & talents:** night-owl, dark-fearing, brine-tolerant, wanderlust, sticky fingers, forge-sense, keen eyes.

**Traits are discovered, not displayed.** New arrivals show a name and a class; the rest you learn by watching them work, eating with them, hearing them out. Attention is the scanner.

### The Key — the "good way"
Every villager has **a Key** derived from their traits: the one adjustment that unlocks them — the right job (your "lazy" salvager is a night-owl on a day schedule), the right companion, a shrine to their god, a grievance answered, a promised Unbinding kept, a day's leave to visit a grave. Find the Key (through the same attention that reveals traits) and they **bloom**: production up, drift resistance up, and their personal Calling unlocks. Bloomed villagers outperform the Broken and the fearful *over any long run* — the good way is mechanically optimal, it just costs the thing the dark way skips: time spent treating people as people.

The blunt instruments stay available and are priced by what they are: **banish** the idle and word travels (banished villagers resurface — in raider camps, in rival stories, occasionally at your gate years later); **execute** and the Shepherd's ledger calls laziness what it is — never a capital crime; **or tolerate** — some people are just surly, and a village that only keeps the optimal is already keeping a ledger it won't like the end of.

### Disposition — tribesmen have inner lives (the RimWorld layer)
Every tribesman carries the traits above **and a hidden disposition**: faith, grievance, and susceptibility, shaped by their history (where you found them matters — reef-survivors and Drop-rescues run susceptible). Disposition **drifts**:

- **Neglect pushes it down** — overwork, no shrine access, bad food, deaths on expeditions you ordered, sleeping in the rain while your hall stands warm.
- **Attention pulls it up** — rest, rites, promotions, grievances heard (talking to your people is gameplay, not flavor).
- **Environment tilts it** — an Unlit priest in the village, insecurely stored remnants, assignments near the Drop.

Drift expresses along a spectrum, **betrayal is only its far end**: slacking → petty theft → spreading doubt (morale is contagious) → desertion → secret dark worship → sabotage (the missing remnant, the defiled shrine) → and finally, if ignored long enough, the full Quiet Ascension arc — a tribesman working Ur-Noth's ends **can hurt the gods for real and, at the limit, affect the Verdict itself** (an unwatched village can lose the ending while you're off hunting legendaries). Everything telegraphs through observable behavior for a player who pays attention; nothing announces itself.

### The Shepherd's ledger — purging is also a deed
The obvious counter-strategy — *execute everyone at the first flicker* — is priced by the same system that prices everything: **the gods watch how you shepherd.** Killing or exiling the innocent dims Halor's blessing, drives priests to leave, dries up pilgrim recruitment (word travels: a butcher keeps that village), and — the elegant part — **fear accelerates drift in the survivors**, breeding exactly the corruption you purged. Tyranny is a self-defeating loop, and the Verdict reads it: a purger is already walking the Ascension's emotional arc (people as resources), and the ending epilogues say so.

The distinction the game enforces is **justice vs. paranoia**: confronting or exiling with evidence (Rite of Revelation, witnessed acts) is legitimate leadership and costs nothing; punishment without evidence is tyranny and compounds. Design law: **the game punishes paranoia, never vigilance.**

Village + church + tribe is the **home half** of the game; the pantheon system makes the home half *mechanically load-bearing* (your magic recovers here), which is the design's quiet best trick.

## Answers to the six open questions (from GAME-SPEC v0.1)
1. **Water traversal: NONE.** The joke is canon — it's all ocean and you walk. Brine pools are hazards, not swimmable. Sailing scope fork: closed.
2. **Style anchors:** Valheim (readable low-fi + weight), Journey/Sable (salt flats + dunes palette discipline), deep-sea documentary bioluminescence for The Drop. One palette per depth band.
3. **Biomes:** Salt Shallows → Reef Forest → The Drop (above).
4. **Afraid of at night:** the Drowned walking up out of the low places, and angler-lights that might be safety and might not. Night = light-management gameplay; the game's face is *a lure in the dark*.
5. **Building fantasy:** wreck-timber homestead → walled village with a church at its heart. Cozy-fortress hybrid; Halor's country.
6. **Progression fantasy:** descent + devotion. Tiers by depth (bronze/reef-iron/tidebrass); identity by how you split devotion across the pantheon + which legends you hunt — respeccable via Rededication, pulled by what you find (gear leads the build), never a class picker.
